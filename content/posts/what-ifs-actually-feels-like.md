Internal Family Systems has had a strange decade. It went from a fringe modality almost nobody had heard of to a TikTok aesthetic and a Selena Gomez interview. The shorthand became *parts work*. The shorthand of the shorthand became *talk to your inner child*.

I'm going to tell you what it actually feels like to do IFS in a session — beyond the caricature.

**The first surprise: nothing dramatic happens at first.**

Most clients arrive expecting a ceremony. They've watched the reels where someone closes their eyes, says "I see you, little one," and weeps. They expect to be guided into something theatrical.

What actually happens: I notice that you've used the same phrase three times in fifteen minutes. *"A part of me knows it's not rational, but…"* I ask what that part is afraid of. You pause. You say something you didn't expect to say.

That's the door.

**The second surprise: you have more than one of you.**

Not in a clinical-fragmentation sense. In an everyday sense. There is the version of you that gets the deck done by Friday. There is the version of you that goes quiet at your mother's house. There is the version of you that picks fights with your partner exactly when intimacy is about to deepen.

These aren't disorders. They're parts. They've been carrying things for you, often since you were nine, often without your conscious permission. IFS asks: *what have they been holding for you, and what would change if they didn't have to hold it alone?*

**The third surprise: the part that's getting in your way is usually a protector.**

Therapy clients want to "fix" the angry part, the avoidant part, the perfectionist part. IFS does the opposite. It thanks them.

I'll often say something like: *"This part has been working very hard, for a long time. What would it be like to ask what it's been protecting?"*

The answer is almost always something tender. The perfectionist is protecting a small, ashamed self who once got laughed at for getting something wrong. The avoidant is protecting a child who learned that asking for closeness ended badly. The angry is protecting the person who couldn't say no when they needed to.

You can't bully these parts into changing. They aren't the problem. They're the immune response to a much older problem.

**Where IFS goes that other modalities don't.**

CBT is excellent for intervening on present-day loops — the catastrophising at 2 a.m., the cognitive distortions, the avoidance patterns. It works at the level of *what you're thinking now.*

IFS works at the level of *who in you is doing the thinking.* It moves slower. It doesn't promise quick wins. It does, often, get to the place that the other approaches kept circling.

For trauma in particular, this matters. A traumatised system isn't going to be argued out of its responses by a worksheet. It needs to be met. It needs to be heard. It needs the part that's been frozen since the original moment to be invited to come out from behind the door.

**Who it's not for.**

If you want techniques to deploy on Monday morning, IFS is going to feel slow. It is slow. That's not a bug.

If you're in acute crisis or active danger, IFS is not the first stop. Stabilisation comes first. Then the deeper work.

If you're philosophically opposed to the idea that the inside of a person is plural — that the self contains multitudes, in Whitman's phrase — IFS will feel like an imposition. Some people genuinely think of themselves as one unified will. I work differently with those clients.

**What it leaves you with.**

Not a transformed self. A more crowded one — but the crowd has stopped fighting. The parts that were exiled get a seat. The parts that were running the show get to rest.

You don't become a different person. You become more of yourself. Less divided.

That, in my experience, is what finally lets the other things in your life start to move.
